How It Works ?

Analysis

Egypt faces multiple grand challenges that hinder its development as a country, with water pollution being the most crucial one. Our project aims to address this pollution by focusing on three parameters:

Salinity through Nanofiltration (NF) Membrane

A membrane is an interfacial structure that restricts the movement of some species while allowing others to permeate if there is a driving force such as pressure, concentration, or chemical potential.

Based on this classification, polymeric membranes were chosen for their outstanding performance and availability in Egypt.
According to research, NF, RO, and Hybrid (NF/RO) membranes achieved average salinity reduction rates of 50.21%, 72.82%, and 78.65% respectively.
In our project, salinity dropped from 1660 ± 166 ppm to 403 ± 40.3 ppm.

Reduction Efficiency Formula:
\[ \text{Efficiency (\%)} = \left(\frac{\text{ConcB - ConcF}}{\text{ConcB}}\right) \times 100 \]
= [(1660 - 403) / 1660] × 100 = 75.7% ± 7.57%

Turbidity through Seashells and Clay

Seashells and clay act as natural biofilters that remove turbidity, odor, and contaminants from wastewater through adsorption and filtration processes.
A study found ≈ 93.8% reduction in turbidity when combining these materials.
In our project, turbidity dropped from 107.2 ± 10.72 NTU to 33.6 ± 3.36 NTU.

Efficiency = [(107.2 - 33.6) / 107.2] × 100 = 68.65% ± 6.86%

pH through NaOH Solution

Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) neutralizes acidic wastewater by providing OH⁻ ions that form water with H⁺ ions.
pH changed from 4.7 ± 0.2 to 7.46 ± 0.3.

pH Efficiency Formula:
\[ \text{pH Efficiency (\%)} = \left(1 - \frac{\text{|pH final - 7.0|}}{\text{|pH initial - 7.0|}}\right) \times 100 \]
= 80% ± 3.4%

Total Reduction Efficiency

Average reduction efficiency = (75.7 + 68.65 + 80) / 3 = 74.8% ± 5.9%

Flow Rate & Power

  • Flow Rate = 2.5 L / 0.5 h = 5 L/h
  • Power = 12 V × 0.35 A = 4.2 W

Energy and Lifetime

Methods

Methods

First of all, sensors were calibrated in the Chemistry Lab using the calibrated devices in there as shown in Figure (1) & Figure (2), reaching these equations as Figure (3) & Figure (4).

Reaching the next point, which is the prototype:

  1. A box was added, as it was the brain of the project where all the sensors were attached.
  2. A hole was made through it, so the valve was inserted.
  3. The first seashell layer was attached below the valve.
  4. Then, the second layer of clay was also attached and the NaOH system was made using a syringe and a nail.
  5. It was connected to the electrodialysis membrane, made in the Faculty of Science at El-Fayoum University to reduce salinity, as shown in Figure (5) & Figure (6).
  6. The purified water was returned to the box using an electric pump to check if additional cycles were needed or not.
  7. All of this was achieved using gravity and pressure made by the pumps. The electric circuit was made as illustrated in Figure (7), and the final prototype is illustrated in Figure (8) & Figure (9).

Why ?

Why?

Water pollution is one of the most critical global challenges. According to the UN:

  • Over 2 billion people globally use a drinking water source contaminated with feces.
  • 80% of wastewater flows back into the ecosystem without being treated or reused.
  • Every day, more than 1,000 children die from diseases linked to unsafe water and poor sanitation.
These statistics reflect the urgent need for affordable and effective water treatment solutions — especially in developing regions.

Our project contributes to addressing these challenges by providing a sustainable, low-cost water purification system. This aligns directly with two of the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs):

  • SDG 6: Clean Water and Sanitation
  • SDG 13: Climate Action
